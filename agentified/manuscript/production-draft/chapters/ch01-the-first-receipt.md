## Chapter 1: The First Receipt

The work item was already assigned when Sam opened the laptop. It sat in the pipeline with a title, an owner, a due date, and a two-sentence summary clean enough to move.

Priority: high. Source: agent. Confidence: high.

Sam read it twice. The recommendation was to add a scoping question to the intake flow for a mid-sized health client, phrased in the client's own vocabulary, tied to a policy line that had shifted the week before. The kind of update a sharp analyst might catch on a quiet Wednesday. It looked correct. It looked useful. It looked ready to ship.

That was the problem.

Nothing sat behind it. No note about which document the policy line came from. No record of who or what had approved the entry. No sign that a person had read the summary before it landed on Priya's list for the day. No path to reverse it if the recommendation turned out to be wrong.

Sam scrolled. Nothing. The item had a result, but no shape.

In Chapter 1 of "Accelerated," "Build Smarter, Move Faster, Stay Human," Sam had spent two years teaching teams that the deck was the deck, the intake was the intake, the metric was the metric, and the story anyone told about the work did not count until the work existed in the world. That principle still held. [A1]

But a second principle sat underneath it now, and Sam had missed it for months.

Once a system can act on behalf of people, the artifact stops being enough. The artifact is what the world sees. The receipt is what the organization needs. Without a receipt, a useful output is indistinguishable from a lucky one, and a lucky one is indistinguishable from a dangerous one.

An artifact had appeared. A system had acted. Priya had a new task. The client would eventually see the intake question, or not. Someone would eventually notice, or not. And Sam could not answer the one question that mattered when a system began acting on behalf of people. Not "did it work." The older question. The one that returned whenever authority moved from a person to a process.

Who did that.

This was where an agentic operating system parted from a demo. Anyone could produce output. The demos were easy. The intake suggestion sitting on Priya's board was a good demo. What separated a demo from a governed operation was a rule Sam had started writing on the back of a notebook page during the ride home the night before.

Traceability before authority.

An agent could draft, route, summarize, recommend, evaluate, and prepare. It could not act on behalf of the company until its work carried a receipt an ordinary teammate could read. Not a log file. Not a JSON blob dumped into a debug pane. A receipt. Something Priya, or the client success lead, or a partner reviewing an audit, could open and understand in under a minute.

Authority without traceability was not authority. It was exposure with better branding.

Sam pulled up a blank canvas and stopped trying to make another table.

The receipt needed to show movement. An agent prepares work. The work enters an evidence envelope. A person reviews the envelope. The outcome ships, changes, or rolls back. The fields mattered, but the motion mattered first. If Priya could not see where the work came from, where it stopped, and who had the authority to move it, the fields would become paperwork instead of protection.

That was the first place Amina's discipline began to take shape: instrument the receipt before negotiating the gate.

The receipt needed nine plain-language fields: intent, agent, source, action, artifact, approval, cost, outcome, and rollback. No mysticism. Just enough evidence for a human to defend the work without becoming the whole workflow.

The pipeline entry that had opened Sam's morning failed on four. Sam rewrote it as an audit he could hand to Priya without a long explanation.

A receipt wraps an agent action in evidence. The output is what the reader sees. The receipt is what the organization needs before the work can earn trust.

The figure did something the clean pipeline card had hidden. It made the risk visible without requiring anyone to be technical. Source, approval, and rollback were missing. Intent was close enough to infer, which made it more dangerous. The card looked complete until someone asked what it would take to defend the work.

Inside Portfolio, the shape of the fix was already visible in the Agent Ops model Sam's team had been sketching for a quarter. Every run produced a run record. Every run record carried events. Events tied to artifacts. Artifacts tied to approvals. Approvals tied to work items. Work items tied to cost events. None of it was decoration. Each link existed so a receipt could be assembled the moment anyone asked, "who did that."

Mission Control could then read receipts across the fleet. The Agent Inbox could hold items missing approval until a person weighed in. The Agent Kanban could park items with missing source citations in a review lane until someone filled the gap. Nothing about it was exotic. It was the operating discipline Sam had once applied to product releases, moved one layer up, so the released thing was the agent's work rather than the team's code.

The receipt was the primitive. Everything else was a view of the receipt.

That was the first product lesson of the agentic layer. Do not start by asking whether the agent is smart. Ask whether the organization can explain the work after the agent is done. If the answer is no, the next feature is not more autonomy. The next feature is a receipt.

Sam wrote Priya a short note, unassigned the pipeline item, and asked the team to hold new agent-produced work items until each one arrived with a receipt. Then Sam opened a fresh page and wrote a single question at the top for the next standup.

Pick one agent action the system took in the last week. Write its receipt in plain language. Intent, agent, source, action, artifact, approval, cost, outcome, rollback. Every field you have to guess is a governance gap. Every gap is a place where a useful output is hiding an unreviewable process.

The intake suggestion was probably correct. That was the seduction of the moment, and Sam felt it. It would have shipped, and it would have helped, and no one would have noticed the missing trail until the day it mattered. That was the day Sam wanted to remove from the calendar.

If an agent acts while you sleep, can you explain what happened before your first meeting? The receipt is the answer.

The learning is still the outcome. The receipt is the trust.
